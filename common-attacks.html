Common Social Engineering Attacks

Understanding the most prevalent manipulation techniques used by attackers

Types of Social Engineering Attacks

Learn to recognize and defend against the most common social engineering tactics

Phishing Attacks

Phishing attacks are digital or voice messages that try to manipulate recipients into sharing sensitive information, downloading malicious software, transferring money or assets to the wrong people or taking some other damaging actions.

Spear Phishing

An attacker researches their target, via LinkedIn, social media, internal workplace, to create personalised messages that look far more credible and legitimate than random mass emails. This has a higher success rate than generic phishing.

Whale Phishing

An attacker targets high level executives or officials, hoping to steal critical information. Attackers usually spend long periods of time researching their victim before launching the attack. A whaling attack typically involves the threat actor impersonating another colleague within the organisation, requesting sensitive information, data or actions from their target. Because whaling attacks target individuals in positions of high rank or power, they often result in a larger payout for the attacker.

Baiting

Baiting is a social engineering attack where the attacker offers something tempting, the "bait"; for example, free software, a prize or access to exclusive content, to lure a victim into taking an unsafe action, such as downloading malware, sharing credentials or using a malicious device.

Baiting exploits human curiosity, greed or the desire for rewards

It exploits human curiosity, greed or the desire for rewards. Baiting is often likened to a Trojan horse in social form as the victim voluntarily brings in the malicious content.

Pretexting

Pretexting is a targeted social engineering technique where an attacker constructs a believable backstory and impersonates a trusted role, e.g. tech support/senior manager, to persuade a target to reveal confidential information or perform insecure actions.

Pretexting relies on creating a convincing false scenario

The attacker creates a fabricated scenario (the pretext) to engage a targeted victim in a way that increases the chance the victim will divulge information or perform actions they wouldn't under ordinary circumstances.
