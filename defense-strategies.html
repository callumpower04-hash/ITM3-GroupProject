How to Spot and Defend Against Social Engineering

Practical strategies to recognize and prevent manipulation attempts

Defending Against Phishing Attacks

Suspicious Emails

Email Warning Signs:

  • Greetings like "Dear Customer" or "Dear Sir/Madam"
  • Posing as Banks or Governing bodies
  • Suspicious email addresses
  • Weird Grammar and spelling errors
  • Attachments and links with embedded code
Pro Tip: Always hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking. Legitimate companies use their official domains.

Spear Phishing Attacks

Personalized Attack Indicators:

  • Referencing specific projects or colleagues
  • Requests for sensitive data via email
  • Unusual payment or transfer requests
  • Emails coming in from "personal" or "non-work emails"
Verification Protocol: Always verify unusual requests through a secondary communication channel (phone call, in-person).

Whaling Attacks

Executive Targeting Signs:

  • Unusual requests from higher ups
  • Strange Financial transactions
  • Confidential data requests via email
  • Impersonation of executives and higher ups
Executive Protection: Systems in place for multi-person approval for sensitive transactions and data access.

Training Verification and Awareness are key to preventing phishing attacks

Countering Baiting Attacks

Digital Baiting Protection

Online Trap Signs:

  • Free software downloads from unofficial sources
  • "Exclusive" content requiring personal information
  • Unexpected prize or reward notifications
  • Requests to disable security software
Download Safety: Only download software from official sources and verified websites.

Physical Security

Real World Use:

  • Do not access sensitive information on public wifi
  • Never leave laptops unattended in a public space
  • Ensure that business data is not stored locally on your machine
  • Never use unknown USB devices
Physical Security: Always lock devices when stepping away and use encrypted storage.

Psychological Awareness

Emotional Manipulation Tactics:

  • Exploiting curiosity or FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out)
  • Leveraging greed or desire for rewards
  • Creating artificial scarcity
  • Playing on helpfulness or desire to assist
Mindful Response: Pause and question offers that trigger strong emotional responses.

Question offers that seem too good to be true - they usually are

Countering Pretexting Attacks

Identity Verification

Impersonation Red Flags:

  • Caller refuses to provide verification
  • Urgent requests bypassing verification channels
  • Inconsistencies in their story
  • Pressure to act immediately and provide them with information
Verification Process: Always call back using official company numbers, not numbers provided by the caller.

Information Control

Information Fishing Signs:

  • Requests for unnecessary Personally Identifiable Information
  • Inquiries about security procedures
  • Asks for assistance with security related questions
Information Policy: Follow company data sharing policies. When in doubt, don't give it out.

Scenario Awareness

Fabricated Scenario Indicators:

  • Overly detailed backstories
  • Manipulation using sympathy or urgency
  • References to non-existent policies
  • Poor technical knowledge
Scenario Testing: Ask detailed questions about their claimed role or situation to test authenticity.

Always verify identities and question unusual scenarios

General Defense Principles

Verify Then Trust

Always verify requests through official channels before taking action

Question Urgency

Legitimate matters rarely require immediate, irreversible action

Protect Information

Share sensitive information only through secure, verified methods

Report Suspicious Activity

Always report potential social engineering attempts to your security team
